# Review: political-autoimmunity

## Verdict
A fair, tough referee would **reject this as an empirical paper but accept-with-major-revisions as a conceptual/measurement-model piece** — provided the author stops dressing stipulation as discovery and fixes two real technical defects. The conceptual core (decompose the folk accusation into gross risk minus a protective offset, then gate by foreseeability and salience) is genuinely clarifying, the literature command is deep and accurate, and the paper is unusually honest about its limits. But every quantitative "finding" is a designed consequence of hand-set inputs, and the marquee result — that a verdict's uncertainty loads onto exposure and spares candidate-hostility — is an **artifact of assigning every factor the same Beta concentration**, so the variance shares are fixed entirely by the stipulated means, not by any modeled difference in how well each input is measured. That gap between what the prose claims and what the code encodes is the single most serious problem.

## Claim grounding
- **"across American voters the probability of voting Republican rises with income, so the picture of the poor voting against material interest while the rich vote for it does not survive contact with the income gradient (Gelman, 2008; Bartels, 2008)."** → **supported** (Gelman's individual-level gradient). Fairly used.
- **"self-interest, measured directly, predicts policy attitudes weakly and inconsistently … (Sears and Funk, 1991)."** → **supported**. Accurate summary of that review.
- **"The model is run on three synthetic but anchored 2024 cases"** (Abstract) → **overstated**. Only the three vote shares are anchored (and the Latino one, 0.46, doesn't match its cited anchor's 0.48). Exposure, dependence, hostility, implementation, magnitude, salience, and awareness — i.e., everything the results turn on — are stipulated. "Anchored" does rhetorical work the data don't earn. §5 is honest about this; the abstract is not.
- **"the verdict's uncertainty is dominated by exposure, salience, and awareness, the quantities an analyst estimates from surveys with the widest error, and is least sensitive to candidate hostility, the quantity an analyst can read off the public record with the most confidence."** (§6) → **unsupported by the model**. The variance decomposition gives every factor the *same* concentration `KAPPA=12`; under fixed concentration, `Var(log Beta)` is a strictly decreasing function of the mean. So each factor's variance share is determined **only** by its stipulated mean (lowest mean → largest share). Exposure tops the list because it was assigned the lowest mean in the dominant cell (0.70), and hostility is near-bottom because it was assigned 0.90 — *not* because hostility is "measured with the most confidence." The "well-measured vs poorly-measured" story is imposed in prose; the code contains no differential measurement error. Tell: in the LGBTQ cell, **D, H, P, M all return exactly 0.0695** because they were all set to 0.90 — confirming the shares track means, not any real-world confidence.
- **"The accusation rests its weight on the input that bears almost none of it."** (§6) → **overstated**. This leans on the 0.07 variance share, which is the artifact above. The paper's *own* tornado tells a milder story: hostility's one-at-a-time swing is 0.343 vs exposure's 0.873 — about 39%, not "almost none."
- **"the net verdict changes sign for one of the three groups"** (Abstract/§6) → **supported but engineered**. The Latino sign flip is produced by a single hand-placed `B = 0.5` in the economy domain plus the material model's 0.55 weight on it. Remove that one stipulated benefit and the flip disappears. It's a valid existence proof that the interest function can flip a verdict; it is not evidence about Latino voters.
- **"The counting frame inverts the most-misaligned group from LGBTQ … to Latino."** (Abstract/§6) → **supported but near-tautological**. It is `vote_share × per-supporter risk`: Latino's share (0.46) is ~3.8× LGBTQ's (0.12) while its per-supporter risk is ~0.39×, so the product inverts. Given the chosen shares the inversion is arithmetically forced. The *conceptual* point (per-voter vs per-bloc) is genuinely worth making; the number is not a finding.
- **Monte-Carlo intervals and survival probabilities (0.743, 0.918, medians 0.396/0.27/0.151, 90% bands)** → **faithfully reported**. Every headline decimal I checked appears in `results.json` (0.743475, 0.91755, 0.4308–0.5844 attrition, E-share 0.2716, H-share 0.0695, tornado E-swing 0.8730, etc.). The prose accurately describes the code.

## Argument
- **§3, the equations don't match the reported numbers (units defect).** The printed definitions carry a vote-share prefactor — $\mathrm{NA}_m = V_{gc}\sum_j W^m_{gj}(B_{gcj}-R_{gcj})$ and $A_m = V_{gc}\sum_j W^m_{gj}\max(0,R-B-\tau)$ — but the code computes both **without** $V_{gc}$ (`net = sum(w*(B-R))`), and §5–§6 report and discuss those sans-$V$ "per supporter" numbers (Latino material net = +0.267 = $\sum W(B-R)$; with $V_{gc}=0.46$ it would be +0.123). A reader recomputing from the paper's own equations gets a number off by a factor of $V_{gc}$. The formulas should either drop $V_{gc}$ or explicitly define the per-supporter variant that the paper actually leads with.
- **§6, the variance-attribution interpretation is an artifact, not a result** (see Claim grounding). The gap: a uniform `KAPPA` makes variance share a deterministic function of the assigned mean, so the "exposure bears the uncertainty, hostility bears none" narrative cannot be read off this model.
- **§6, robustness of the LGBTQ case is partly manufactured.** All three LGBTQ domains are tagged `type="rights"`, so the five type-weighted interest models normalize to identical (1/3, 1/3, 1/3) weights and return *literally the same* score (−0.27309 for material, rights, expressive, coalition-entry, protest). The "LGBTQ line barely moves across seven interest functions" is therefore partly because the synthetic data gave it homogeneous domain types, making the interest function inoperative — not only "no protective domain" as the text says. The most robust headline claim sits on this.
- **§6, independence assumption undefended.** The Monte-Carlo draws E, D, H, P, M, S, F, a as mutually independent Betas. Exposure and dependence within a domain, and public-foreseeability vs voter-awareness, are surely correlated; independence is what makes the variance decompose cleanly, so the cleanliness is assumed, not earned. One sentence of defense (or a sensitivity check) is owed.
- **What is genuinely strong:** the identity $\mathrm{NA}_m = \Pi_m - G_m$ and the observation that the folk accusation observes a slice of $G_m$ and silently sets $\Pi_m=0$ is a real, compact, correct insight — the best thing in the paper. The foreseeability/salience gates yielding a typology (uninformed adverse-interest / informed tradeoff / protest / miscalculated protest) is a useful reframe with honest literature behind it (Zaller, Converse, Kuklinski, Key). The reflexive stance — the model reports which of its own assumptions a verdict depends on before issuing one — is principled and well executed in §7.

## Factual errors
No hard factual errors found. The closest things are (1) the Latino input 0.46 vs the cited Pew anchor's 48% — a small inconsistency, mitigated by the "order of magnitude" hedge; and (2) the §3 equations carrying a $V_{gc}$ factor that the reported per-supporter numbers omit — a definitional/units error rather than a factual one, but it means the printed formulas do not reproduce the paper's own headline numbers. Both are fixable in revision. All scholarly attributions, dates, and venues I checked are correct.

## Prose and insight
**Alive:** "Multiplication is the model's way of refusing to be frightened by any one number." "the ecological fallacy with a coat of arithmetic." "the gate rewards a candidate's transparency about hostility by keeping more of it on the ledger." The closing — "a model that claimed otherwise would be the propaganda tool the construct was built to avoid becoming" — lands. The abstract's opening ("to say a group voted against its interests is to run a calculation and hide most of its inputs") is a genuinely good thesis sentence. The autoimmunity metaphor is apt and not overworked.

**Dead:** §2's fourth paragraph is a citation cascade (Campbell → Tajfel → Mason → Iyengar → Jost → Sidanius → Hajnal → Mutz → Cramer → Hochschild in one breath) that reads as a literature dump establishing erudition rather than argument; a reader cannot retain it. The "Three further readings name specific contents…" stretch similarly recites more than it reasons.

**Genuine contribution:** the $\Pi - G$ decomposition and the reframing of "voted against interests" as a comparison with a half the accusation omits. That is real and portable. The synthetic computation, by contrast, contributes illustration, not evidence — and the paper would be stronger if it said so even more plainly and stopped letting "anchored," "computed," and "the finding" imply otherwise.

## Top fixes
1. **Stop the variance-attribution overclaim, or model what you claim.** Either (a) explicitly state that under uniform `KAPPA` the variance shares are a deterministic function of the stipulated means and carry no information about real measurement error — so "exposure is measured worst, hostility best" is an interpretive overlay, not a result; or (b) actually encode differential confidence by giving each factor its own concentration (high `KAPPA` for hostility read off the record, low for survey-estimated exposure) and re-run. As written, the claim is unsupported by the model.
2. **Fix the §3 equations.** The printed $\mathrm{NA}_m$ and $A_m$ include a $V_{gc}$ prefactor that the reported per-supporter numbers drop. Define the per-supporter quantity (sans $V_{gc}$) and the population-weighted quantity ($\times V_{gc}$) separately, and make the equation paired with each §6 number actually reproduce that number.
3. **Disclose that the LGBTQ "robustness across interest functions" is partly structural.** Note that all three LGBTQ domains share `type="rights"`, so the five type-weighted models are mathematically identical; report how the result changes if even one domain is re-typed. The single most-robust claim in the paper currently rests on a homogeneity in the synthetic data.
4. **Demote "anchored" in the abstract.** Say plainly that only three vote shares touch data (and that the Latino 0.46 differs from its Pew anchor's 0.48), and that all risk-bearing inputs are stipulated to display the model's geometry. Let the honesty of §5/§7 reach the abstract.
5. **Defend or test the independence assumption** in the Monte-Carlo, with at least one correlated-draw sensitivity check (E–D within domain; F–a within the gate), since the clean variance decomposition depends on it.
6. **Trim §2's citation cascades** into argument: for each interest function, one sentence on what it would change in a verdict, rather than a roster of supporting authors.
7. **Reconcile the two sensitivity framings.** The variance decomposition (H = 0.07, "almost none") and the tornado (H-swing = 0.34, ~39% of exposure's) tell different stories; pick the honest one and don't let "almost none" borrow from the artifact while the tornado says otherwise.
