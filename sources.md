# Sources

The frozen bibliography. Each entry in the form it takes in the paper's `## References`, with a one-line provenance note. See the workspace docs (`papers docs`): research-pipeline.md §4. Every in-text `et al.` maps to a co-authored entry here.

Refs-gate note: all entries are author-year, so the gate is precise (cited resolves to bib and back). Data-source entries are written with the organization as the lead author (`Pew Research Center (2025)`, `Council on American-Islamic Relations (2024)`) so the year-pattern and lead-token match; they anchor the vote-share inputs only, flagged in-text by source quality.

## Bibliography

Achen, C. H., and Bartels, L. M. (2016). *Democracy for Realists: Why Elections Do Not Produce Responsive Government*. Princeton University Press. — the folk theory of democracy and its dismantling; retrospective myopia; the case against reading vote choice as considered interest (§1, §6).

Bartels, L. M. (2008). *Unequal Democracy: The Political Economy of the New Gilded Age*. Princeton University Press. — the empirical record on income, policy, and vote; a check on the material-interest model (§2, §5).

Brennan, G., and Lomasky, L. (1993). *Democracy and Decision: The Pure Theory of Electoral Preference*. Cambridge University Press. — expressive voting; the strongest challenge to any external interest model, since symbolic utility counts as real (§2).

Campbell, A., Converse, P. E., Miller, W. E., and Stokes, D. E. (1960). *The American Voter*. Wiley. — the Michigan model; party identification as the standing prior on vote choice (§2).

Caplan, B. (2007). *The Myth of the Rational Voter: Why Democracies Choose Bad Policies*. Princeton University Press. — rational irrationality; the low cost of expressive error at the ballot (§2, §4).

Converse, P. E. (1964). The nature of belief systems in mass publics. In D. E. Apter (Ed.), *Ideology and Discontent* (pp. 206–261). Free Press. — non-attitudes and the thinness of issue constraint; bears on the awareness gate (§4).

Council on American-Islamic Relations. (2024). *CAIR Exit Poll of Muslim Voters: 2024 General Election*. CAIR. — advocacy-survey exit poll; the Muslim vote-share anchor, flagged as not validated-voter quality (§5).

Cramer, K. J. (2016). *The Politics of Resentment: Rural Consciousness in Wisconsin and the Rise of Scott Walker*. University of Chicago Press. — the expressive and status economy of resentment, observed ethnographically (§2).

Crenshaw, K. (1991). Mapping the margins: Intersectionality, identity politics, and violence against women of color. *Stanford Law Review*, 43(6), 1241–1299. — intersectionality; why a group's "interest" is not additive across identities (§1, §7).

Dawson, M. C. (1994). *Behind the Mule: Race and Class in African-American Politics*. Princeton University Press. — linked fate; the heuristic that substitutes for individual interest computation (§2).

Downs, A. (1957). *An Economic Theory of Democracy*. Harper & Row. — the spatial model and the calculus of voting; the instrumental baseline the paper departs from (§2).

Elster, J. (1985). *Making Sense of Marx*. Cambridge University Press. — the analytical-Marxist treatment of false consciousness; what separates it from measured misalignment (§1).

Festinger, L. (1957). *A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance*. Stanford University Press. — dissonance reduction; the mechanism behind post-vote rationalization, distinguished from misalignment (§1, §7).

Fiorina, M. P. (1981). *Retrospective Voting in American National Elections*. Yale University Press. — retrospective voting; protest as rational punishment of incumbents (§4).

Frank, T. (2004). *What's the Matter with Kansas? How Conservatives Won the Heart of America*. Metropolitan Books. — the popular statement of the "voting against interests" thesis the paper formalizes and qualifies (§1).

Gelman, A. (2008). *Red State, Blue State, Rich State, Poor State: Why Americans Vote the Way They Do*. Princeton University Press. — the income-vote gradient; the empirical rebuttal to the crude Frank thesis (§1, §5).

Graham, J., Haidt, J., and Nosek, B. A. (2009). Liberals and conservatives rely on different sets of moral foundations. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 96(5), 1029–1046. — moral foundations; religious and authority values as genuine, not erroneous, preference structure (§2).

Green, D. P., Palmquist, B., and Schickler, E. (2002). *Partisan Hearts and Minds: Political Parties and the Social Identities of Voters*. Yale University Press. — partisanship as a stable social identity, not a running policy tally (§2).

Hajnal, Z. L., and Lee, T. (2011). *Why Americans Don't Join the Party: Race, Immigration, and the Failure of Political Parties to Engage the Electorate*. Princeton University Press. — minority partisanship, non-identification, and coalition entry (§2, §5).

Hochschild, A. R. (2016). *Strangers in Their Own Land: Anger and Mourning on the American Right*. New Press. — the "deep story"; status and belonging as the felt content of a vote (§2).

Iyengar, S., Lelkes, Y., Levendusky, M., Malhotra, N., and Westwood, S. J. (2019). The origins and consequences of affective polarization in the United States. *Annual Review of Political Science*, 22, 129–146. — affective polarization; out-party animus as a vote driver independent of policy (§2).

Iyengar, S., Sood, G., and Lelkes, Y. (2012). Affect, not ideology: A social identity perspective on polarization. *Public Opinion Quarterly*, 76(3), 405–431. — the foundational measurement of partisan affect (§2).

Jost, J. T., and Banaji, M. R. (1994). The role of stereotyping in system-justification and the production of false consciousness. *British Journal of Social Psychology*, 33(1), 1–27. — system justification; out-group favoritism among the disadvantaged (§2).

Jost, J. T., Banaji, M. R., and Nosek, B. A. (2004). A decade of system justification theory. *Political Psychology*, 25(6), 881–919. — the consolidated evidence for system-justifying motivation (§2).

Key, V. O. (1966). *The Responsible Electorate: Rationality in Presidential Voting, 1936–1960*. Harvard University Press. — "voters are not fools"; retrospective rationality as a defense of the electorate (§4).

Kinder, D. R., and Kam, C. D. (2009). *Us Against Them: Ethnocentric Foundations of American Opinion*. University of Chicago Press. — ethnocentrism as an organizing disposition in opinion (§2).

Kinder, D. R., and Sanders, L. M. (1996). *Divided by Color: Racial Politics and Democratic Ideals*. University of Chicago Press. — racial resentment; the contested measurement that mixes prejudice and ideology (§2).

Kuklinski, J. H., Quirk, P. J., Jerit, J., Schwieder, D., and Rich, R. F. (2000). Misinformation and the currency of democratic citizenship. *Journal of Politics*, 62(3), 790–816. — confident misinformation, distinct from ignorance; bears on the awareness gate (§4).

Lazarsfeld, P. F., Berelson, B., and Gaudet, H. (1944). *The People's Choice: How the Voter Makes Up His Mind in a Presidential Campaign*. Columbia University Press. — cross-pressure; conflicting group memberships as the normal condition, not the exception (§2, §7).

Lupia, A., and McCubbins, M. D. (1998). *The Democratic Dilemma: Can Citizens Learn What They Need to Know?* Cambridge University Press. — information shortcuts; how voters reason with little information (§4).

Mason, L. (2018). *Uncivil Agreement: How Politics Became Our Identity*. University of Chicago Press. — identity sorting; the stacking of social identities onto party (§2).

Miller, A. H., Gurin, P., Gurin, G., and Malanchuk, O. (1981). Group consciousness and political participation. *American Journal of Political Science*, 25(3), 494–511. — group consciousness as the condition under which group exposure becomes a voter priority (§4).

Mutz, D. C. (2018). Status threat, not economic hardship, explains the 2016 presidential vote. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 115(19), E4330–E4339. — status threat; the expressive-status interest model's empirical anchor and its critics (§2).

Pew Research Center. (2025). *Behind Trump's 2024 Victory, a More Racially and Ethnically Diverse Voter Coalition*. Pew Research Center. — validated-voter estimates; the Latino vote-share anchor and its quality baseline (§5).

Riker, W. H., and Ordeshook, P. C. (1968). A theory of the calculus of voting. *American Political Science Review*, 62(1), 25–42. — the P·B − C + D calculus; the D term that makes expressive voting tractable (§2).

Saltelli, A., Ratto, M., Andres, T., Campolongo, F., Cariboni, J., Gatelli, D., Saisana, M., and Tarantola, S. (2008). *Global Sensitivity Analysis: The Primer*. Wiley. — variance-based sensitivity; the method behind the attribution and tornado analyses (§6).

Sears, D. O., and Funk, C. L. (1991). The role of self-interest in social and political attitudes. *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 24, 1–91. — the repeated finding that self-interest weakly predicts policy attitudes; the empirical ground for plural interests (§2).

Sen, A. (1977). Rational fools: A critique of the behavioral foundations of economic theory. *Philosophy & Public Affairs*, 6(4), 317–344. — the interest, preference, and commitment distinction; the conceptual spine of seven interests, not one (§2, §3).

Shapley, L. S. (1953). A value for n-person games. In H. W. Kuhn and A. W. Tucker (Eds.), *Contributions to the Theory of Games, Volume II* (pp. 307–317). Princeton University Press. — the value, applied reflexively to attribute the model's own output among its input factors (§6).

Sidanius, J., and Pratto, F. (1999). *Social Dominance: An Intergroup Theory of Social Hierarchy and Oppression*. Cambridge University Press. — social dominance orientation; hierarchy preference as a measurable motive (§2).

Stenner, K. (2005). *The Authoritarian Dynamic*. Cambridge University Press. — authoritarian predisposition activated by threat; order as a genuine, not pathological, interest (§2).

Tajfel, H., and Turner, J. C. (1979). An integrative theory of intergroup conflict. In W. G. Austin and S. Worchel (Eds.), *The Social Psychology of Intergroup Relations* (pp. 33–47). Brooks/Cole. — social identity theory; the in-group and out-group machinery behind expressive and status motives (§2).

Tversky, A., and Kahneman, D. (1981). The framing of decisions and the psychology of choice. *Science*, 211(4481), 453–458. — framing; why salience and awareness are constructed, not given, which the gate operationalizes (§4).

Zaller, J. R. (1992). *The Nature and Origins of Mass Opinion*. Cambridge University Press. — the receive-accept-sample model; elite cues and the supply side of foreseeability (§4).
